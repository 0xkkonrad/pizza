We queued for fifty minutes in a rain that never quite decided to fall. Natalia had formed opinions about the queue long before we reached the door — about the couple ahead of us splitting one margherita "to stay light," about the man photographing his pizza from a chair he'd dragged into the street. By the time we sat down at a shared marble table we were starving, and starving is the best sauce, which rather complicates the reviewing.

It came fast, folded, on a paper plate, and it was very good in the plain, unbothered way the great ones are — no flourish, no argument, just a base that knew exactly what it was for. The char came gentle, more spot than smoke, and the tomatoes did the singing.

The oil did nothing at all. We kept adding it, hoping, the way you do. Then we walked out into the same undecided rain, already arguing gently about where it ranked — and whether "normal," for the beauty of the thing, had been too plain a word for something we would both, quietly, want again.
